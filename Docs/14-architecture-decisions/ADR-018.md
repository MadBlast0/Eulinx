# ADR-018: Orchestrators Plan; the Runtime Is Deterministic

## Status

Accepted.

## Context

Eulinx has a hierarchy of orchestrators (root, phase, task) that plan work, decompose goals, spawn workers, and rewrite plans when reality changes — genuinely AI-driven reasoning. It also has runtime services — Scheduler, MergeManager, LockManager, PermissionManager, MemoryManager, ArtifactManager, ToolRegistry, EventBus, WorkspaceManager — that schedule, enforce locks, merge patches, check permissions, and track resources. A design mistake would be to blur these: to have an LLM decide scheduling order, or to hard-code planning into a deterministic service. Using a model to do work that can be done algorithmically wastes tokens (fatal to the cheap-model economics), introduces nondeterminism into the parts that must be reproducible, and makes the system harder to reason about and to replay.

The two kinds of work have opposite requirements: planning benefits from intelligence and tolerates nondeterminism; infrastructure demands determinism, auditability, and zero token cost.

## Decision

Eulinx keeps a hard separation between the **AI layer** and the **runtime layer**. Orchestrators and workers *reason and plan* — they decompose goals, choose how many workers to spawn, and rewrite plans. Runtime services are *deterministic and do not call an LLM* — they schedule work, enforce locks, merge patches, evaluate permissions, assemble context, and track resources by algorithm. No runtime service uses a model to make its decision; no planning logic is hard-coded into a runtime service. This is why the WorkflowEngine (deterministic interpreter) is separate from the reasoning that fills a graph (ADR-006), and it is what makes deterministic replay (ADR-020) possible.

## Consequences

Positive:

- Tokens are spent only on reasoning that needs intelligence; scheduling, locking, and merging cost nothing and run fast, protecting the cost model.
- The deterministic layer is testable and reproducible, which is a precondition for replay and for trustworthy behavior.
- The system is easier to reason about: "did an AI decide this?" has a clear answer per component.

Negative:

- The boundary must be policed; it is tempting to "just ask the model" to resolve a scheduling or merge decision, which would poison determinism and cost.
- Some decisions sit near the line (e.g., conflict resolution strategy); the design must decide clearly whether each is deterministic policy or delegated reasoning, and default to deterministic.
- Deterministic services can be less "smart" than an LLM in edge cases; they must fail closed and, where genuinely needed, surface the decision to an orchestrator or the human rather than guessing.

## Alternatives Considered

- **A single AI orchestrator that also schedules, locks, and merges.** Rejected because it spends tokens on algorithmic work, injects nondeterminism into infrastructure, and cannot be replayed.
- **Fully deterministic everything, including planning.** Rejected because decomposition, dynamic worker counts, and replanning are exactly where model intelligence adds value; hard-coding them removes Eulinx's adaptivity.
- **No explicit layering; decide per feature.** Rejected because without a stated rule the boundary erodes over time and the cost/determinism guarantees quietly disappear.
